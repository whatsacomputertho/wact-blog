Background

During my time at Penn State, I hesitated to fully commit to a mathematics degree.  By the time I did finally commit to pursuing a math degree, I only had enough time to pursue a minor in mathematics.  As a result, I felt as though I missed out on some of the many essential topics in mathematics beyond those I had the opportunity to study in college.  One such topic is analysis.

To ​compensate ​for ​this, ​I ​ended ​up ​buying ​a ​few ​textbooks that I had planned to self-study and learn more about the continuous mathematics that I had missed out on in college.  But of course, life gets in the way, and these books ended up sitting on my bookshelf for a few years.  That is, until recently when I decided enough was enough, and I was finally going to commit to learning real analysis.

Many who have brushed the surface of mathematics likely have some top-down understanding of real analysis.  This is because various results from real analysis serve as the theoretical underpinnings of calculus, such as continuity, differentiability, and who can forget, the fundamental theorem of calculus.  But these results, albeit extremely practical, do not exactly lie at the very core of real analysis.  Otherwise we might call real analysis "the theory of calculus".

So if real analysis is not a rigorous theory of calculus, then what is it?

What's ​Real Analysis, Though?

Real analysis at its core answers a very simple question: what are the real numbers?  We all have some rough understanding of what real numbers are.  We know that real numbers can be written out using decimal notation, such as

\pi = 3.141592...

We ​also ​know that \sqrt{2} is a real number,  5 is a real number, and \frac{2}{3} is a real number.  We also know that a function like f(x) = x^{2} can be plotted on the real plane  \mathbb{R}^{2} like so.

But ​nonetheless, ​we ​lack ​a rigorous understanding of the real numbers.  Our present understanding has been built upon a foundation of intuition and not a logically sound one.  Real analysis provides the logically sound foundation atop which our intuitive understanding of the real numbers can rest safely.  This, of course, includes the extremely useful applications of the mathematical infinite, such as calculus.​  As it turns out, when dealing with the concept of infinity in mathematics, one can encounter counterintuitive logical pitfalls which can only be navigated with the help of a rigorous logical foundation.

So, yes, in some sense analysis does provide a "theory of calculus".  Calculus is ultimately the core value proposition that is meant to be derived atop its logical foundation.  But analysis also deals with many mystical properties of the real numbers which are often ignored from the more utilitarian perspective of calculus.

Ultimately, ​the study of real analysis converges (pun intended) at a construction of the real numbers which is logically rooted in the more well-known constructions of the natural numbers \mathbb{N}
 , the integers  \mathbb{Z} , and the rational numbers \mathbb{Q} .  The most noteworthy part of this construction is the fact that no new axioms are incurred in its production.

In other words, one may show that the existence of the rational numbers necessarily imply the existence of the real numbers.​

What's 'What's real analysis, though' though?

My goal with this blog series is simply to recount my reading of my analysis textbook with a focus on the mystical and elusive properties of the real numbers rather than the utilitarian aspect of deriving calculus.  I do not intend for this to serve as a perfectly self-contained explainer series on analysis.  Instead, I'd like to simply talk about some of the interesting things I read, as well as some questions that arise in my mind upon reading them.

For reference, the textbook I am reading is Understanding Analysis by Stephen Abbott (PDF, Buy, Solutions). ​ ​If interested, you might read along with me, and treat my blog series as my pre-chapter commentary on the book.
